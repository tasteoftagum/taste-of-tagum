Street Food

Why Tagum City Has the Best Street Food You've Never Heard Of

The vendors appear every afternoon like magic. The city gathers. Five pesos buys you a memory that lasts decades.

By Daniel Troy Socorro May 2026 Tagum City, Davao del Norte

Your cover photo here — shoot the street food stalls on Rizal Street
Shot on iPhone 11 Pro Max

There is a version of Tagum City that tourists don't see in brochures.

It doesn't happen in the morning. It doesn't happen inside restaurants. It happens at around 4 or 5 in the afternoon, when the heat starts to soften and the city exhales after a long day — and suddenly, like magic, the street vendors appear.

Stalls unfold along Rizal Street, in the shadow of what is now the Tagum City Historical and Cultural Center. Smoke rises from hot oil. The smell of deep frying proben drifts down the sidewalk. Someone's squeezing buko juice. Kids are running between stalls. Jeepneys rumble past. Shoe cleaners set up beside the food vendors. Kutsero vendors call out to passersby, telling anyone who will listen about how their day went.

"This is the heart of Tagum. Not the festivals, not the government buildings. This. Right here. Five pesos and a plastic stool and the sun going down."

The Place That Raised Us

The old City Hall of Tagum has since been transformed into a cultural and historical center — now home to the Kagikan Museum of the Tagumenyo People, a city library, an art gallery, and the Tagum Theater, with a statue of Jose Rizal standing out front on Rizal Street.

It is beautiful. It is worth visiting. The city should be proud of it.

But for those of us who grew up in Tagum, that corner of the city will always mean something else first. It will always mean the afternoon vendors. The smell of the oil. The sound of the city winding down. The moment when Tagum stopped being busy and started being itself.

Those vendors have been part of this city longer than most of us can remember. The buildings changed around them. The vendors adapted. They are still there.

Proben: The Street Food That Has No Equal

If you have never had proben, I genuinely feel sorry for you.

Proben is processed chicken proventriculus — the part of a chicken's digestive system that most people wouldn't think twice about eating. The vendors skewer it, drop it in hot oil, and somehow turn something humble into something that stops you in your tracks.

Close up photo of proben on skewer
with suka tuba dipping sauce

Proben — five pesos a stick. The soul of Tagum street food.

Five pesos a stick. A tiny packet of rice wrapped in cellophane for another five. And the dipping sauce — suka tuba, the local coconut vinegar with crushed garlic and chili — is the kind of thing that stays in your memory for years.

I know this because it stayed in mine.

My mother used to buy me four sticks of proben every time we passed through on our way home. Four sticks. Not three, not five — four, because she knew exactly how much would make me happy. One of the stalls belonged to my uncle, and I remember running toward it the moment I could smell the oil from down the street. That smell — hot oil, vinegar, charcoal — is one of the clearest memories I have of growing up in this city.

"That's what street food does when it's done right. It stops being food and starts being a memory you carry around for the rest of your life."

What You'll Find at Rizal Street

If you visit Tagum and want to experience the real afternoon street food culture, here is what to look for:

  • Proben — skewered chicken proventriculus, deep fried, served with suka tuba. Non-negotiable first stop.
  • Kwek-kwek — orange battered quail eggs, also deep fried, also served with vinegar dipping sauce. A Filipino street food classic that Tagum does particularly well.
  • Maruya — banana fritters, slightly sweet, crispy on the outside. Perfect for the walk home.
  • Fishball and squid ball — on a stick, sauced, five pesos each, endlessly satisfying.
  • Isaw — grilled chicken intestines. Smoky, chewy, absolutely worth it.
  • Buko juice — fresh young coconut, cold, served straight from the shell. The perfect counter to all that fried food.

Why This Matters

Tagum is many things. It is the most populous component city in Mindanao, one of the most competitive mid-sized cities in the Philippines, and has been called the Cultural Center of Mindanao.

But long before the awards and the accolades and the beautiful new cultural center on Rizal Street — Tagum was a city where people gathered in the afternoon, ate five-peso proben with vinegar, and watched the sun go down together.

That version of Tagum is still there. It just doesn't make the brochures.

This site exists to find it, document it, and share it — one bite at a time.
